ABOUT PIANO

a piano, while not literally a physical end, refers to when an instrument is no longer considered playable or cost-effective to restore. This typically happens after 80-90 years due to deterioration of materials like wood, tuning pins, and strings, along with wear and tear. Whether an instrument is considered "beyond repair" also depends on the economics of restoring it versus purchasing a new one. Here's a more detailed breakdown:

1. Physical Deterioration:

Wood:

Wood can crack and weaken, especially with changes in humidity and temperature.

Tuning Pins:

Tuning pins can loosen, affecting the piano's ability to be tuned.

Felt and Leather:

Felt and leather parts wear thin and deteriorate, impacting the piano's tone and action.

Strings:

Strings can break, leading to a loss of sound and requiring replacement.

2. Economic Factors:

Cost of Repair:

The cost of restoring an old piano to playable condition may exceed the cost of purchasing a new one of comparable quality.

Market Value:

Some older pianos may be worth more as historical artifacts than as playable instruments, even if repaired.

3. "Death" vs. "Afterlife":

Piano Disposals:

In recent years, there's been an increase in piano disposals, coinciding with the peak sales of new instruments in the early 20th century.

Creative Re-purposing:

Some artists are finding creative ways to use "dead" pianos in their work, such as destructive performances.

Historical Significance:

Old pianos can be valuable historical artifacts, even if they are no longer playable.

4. Examples of "Piano Death" in Practice:

Discarded Pianos:

Some artists and musicians use discarded pianos in their performances, sometimes destroying them as part of the performance.

Sound Libraries:

Sample libraries like "Death Piano" use the sounds of old, often distressed, pianos to create unique and often unsettling sounds.
