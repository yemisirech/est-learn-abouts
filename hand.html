HAND

The human hand is a complex structure primarily used for grasping, manipulating objects, and sensing touch. It consists of 27 bones, including the 14 phalanges (finger bones), five metacarpals, and eight carpal bones (wrist bones). The fingers are rich in nerve endings, providing a high degree of tactile feedback and allowing for precise movements.

Here's a more detailed breakdown:

Structure:

Digits:

The human hand typically has five digits: four fingers and one thumb. These are often referred to collectively as five fingers.

Bones:

The hand contains 27 bones, excluding the sesamoid bone, which varies in number between individuals.

Phalanges:

The 14 phalanges (proximal, intermediate, and distal) form the bones of the fingers and thumb.

Metacarpals:

The five metacarpals connect the fingers to the carpal bones of the wrist.

Carpals:

The eight carpal bones form the wrist.

Function:

Grasping and Manipulation:

Hands are crucial for grasping, holding, and manipulating objects, enabling a wide range of activities.

Tactile Sensation:

Fingers are highly sensitive to touch, providing a rich source of tactile feedback.

Movement and Precision:

The hands and fingers have great positioning capability, allowing for precise movements and delicate tasks.

Brain Control:

Each hand is dominantly controlled by the opposite brain hemisphere, influencing handedness (preference for using one hand over the other).

Interesting Facts:

Injuries:

Upper extremity injuries, including those to the hand and wrist, are common, accounting for a significant portion of acute injuries seen in emergency rooms.

Work Injuries:

The most common disabling work injuries in the US often involve the upper extremities, with finger injuries being a notable component.

Handedness:

Handedness (right or left-handed) is a reflection of individual brain functioning and the dominance of one hemisphere.
